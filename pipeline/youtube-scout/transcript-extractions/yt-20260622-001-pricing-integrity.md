## Core insight

Discounting because a buyer tells a story creates resentment later. The real problem is not whether the client had more money. The problem is whether BridgeWorks agreed to a price it did not actually stand behind.

For Emmanuel, this matters because BridgeWorks is solo, cash-constrained, and prone to over-delivery. The guardrail should be simple: if a discount is given, it must be a deliberate business decision with a written trade-off, not a reaction to pressure, sympathy, or fear of losing the lead.

## BridgeWorks adaptation

Add a pricing integrity check before sending offers or accepting scope changes:

1. What is the normal price?
2. What is the exact discount or concession?
3. What does BridgeWorks receive in exchange?
4. What scope is removed or delayed?
5. Would Emmanuel still be at peace with this price if the client later proved wealthier than expected?
6. Is this a one-time strategic exception or a new pricing rule?

## Use now

- Oliviks: use for any extra catering page, CMS, cart, or delivery feature discussion. Do not quietly add scope because it feels small.
- Foundation package: document what EUR 750 includes and what becomes add-on work.
- BridgeWorks outreach: qualify ability to pay before building large free audits.

## Reject / risk

Do not turn this into harsh sales posture. BridgeWorks can still be generous. The rule is not `never discount`. The rule is `never discount without a clear exchange and scope boundary`.
